About

I hold a Bachelor of Science in Industrial Design with a specialization in Graphic Design from Universidade Estácio de Sá, Rio de Janeiro, and a postgraduate certificate in Industrial and Organizational Psychology.

My professional practice spans a decade of client work across branding, print, digital, and editorial media in Brazil, alongside ongoing independent design and a published poetry anthology credit in 2024.

For the past several years, my primary creative project has been an epic fantasy novel set in the world of Ethernos — a large-scale narrative system with original cosmology, a hard magic framework, and a cast of characters navigating civilization-scale stakes. I am interested in how computational tools and interactive media can extend, complicate, and deepen the practice of narrative world-building.

Original Verse · Epic Fantasy · Ethernos

The Song of Ymaitá

A creation myth from the world of Ethernos, written in the oral tradition of the forest peoples

This poem is an in-world artefact from an ongoing epic fantasy novel. It functions as a creation myth for a forest civilization, tracing the origin of their people through the love between a mortal healer and the god Sylvarius. Written in an English oral-tradition cadence, it was composed as part of the novel's world-building practice — an attempt to build culture from the inside out, through the texts a people might actually carry.

Before the first of us was born, The forest breathed without a morn. The silent trees spoke to none, Neither wind nor empty stone.
The living things would grow and fall, Without a name or home at all. And no one held their memory, Or marked their place in history.
Then she was born and he took heed, Her soul was bright in word and deed. For Sylvarius watched her shine, A mortal spark of pure design.
Her name was lost in distant years, Before the trees had shed their fears. She walked upon the broken ground, To heal the wound and what was bound.
She laid her hands on wounded bark, To light a fire within the dark. The old and dying heard her call, And green began to bloom for all.
We call her Ymaitá the old, The one the forest came to hold. We call her Yamay, made of light, A name to carry through the night.
The god had heard her spirit first, From root and leaf and salty thirst. Between the falling of the leaf, And where it came to find relief.
He listened from the deepest place, To see the glory of her face. He came because a simple truth, Can pull a god to mortal youth.
He wore a shape she would not fear, To walk beside her, always near. Through gold and green and misty dew, He learned of things he never knew.
He learned the ache of hunger's bread, He learned the weight of mortal dread. He learned to love a thing of gold, That wind could take but he could hold.
Their love kept every thicket bound, Where life was fierce and deep and sound. The roots reached far beneath the clay, The branches stretched to meet the day.
But she was mortal, time was fast, A shadow on the field was cast. The turning seasons, iron-clad, Left Sylvarius dark and sad.
For soon would come the winter grey, Her silver hair would fade away. He knew that leaves must always fall, And death would come to claim them all.
But seasons care not for a god, Nor for the path where feet have trod. There came a day her breath was drawn, And left him in a grey-eyed dawn.
He grieved a pain so deep and new, It tore his ageless heart in two. He wept a sea for her alone, That cut through root and mountain stone.
From every drop that hit the floor, A thousand rushing rivers bore. He buried her with his own hands, Who shaped the seas and all the lands.
He gave the earth his love and grief, To buy the soil a small relief. And there he stayed, a silent guard, Upon the ground his tears had scarred.
For he had learned a truth that day: That love will never walk away. He waited past the count of days, Through frost and flood and silent gaze.
Then, slow as only true things grow, A tree rose from the dirt below. It reached for light with holy grace, To claim its home and hallowed place.
Within a season with no name, A single, prideful blossom came. It opened to the stars at night, A silver bloom of sacred light.
From it there stepped a child of earth, Of moss and rain and holy birth. The first of us was born that day, And Sylvarius cleared the way.
He wept for love and not for grief, For she was born without being brief. She had her mother's face and hands, Beyond the reach of death's commands.
For his own blessing shaped her soul, To make the broken spirit whole. He called her Ymaitá the old, The one the woods were meant to hold.
He called her Yamay, what she was, Beneath the weight of ancient laws. "You are what's left," he whispered low, "When love refuses grief to grow."
We have not forgotten. We will not forget. Our roots have never withered. And we still remember, yet.

Epic Fantasy Novel · Ethernos · First Draft

Chapter 19

Hearth of the Frostlands — from an ongoing novel set in the world of Ethernos

This chapter introduces the northern city of Frosthelm and is presented here as a working draft. The novel follows a diverse ensemble cast working against time to prevent a cataclysm threatening the world of Ethernos. This chapter was chosen for the portfolio because it demonstrates character voice, ensemble dynamics, cultural world-building through immersive detail, and narrative tension without reliance on action — skills I am most interested in developing through graduate research.
